# User research 

## Target audience
The user research protocol should focus on understanding how different people interpret the interconnection bottleneck, whether they see it as a PJM-specific failure or a broader national grid problem, and which parts of the story are most persuasive, confusing, or memorable. It should also identify whether the sequence of the presentation — from scale to bottleneck to PJM to nationwide implications — helps people follow the argument and accept the need for action.

To identify representative individuals, I would look for classmates with a range of backgrounds and familiarity levels: some who know a little about energy or policy, some who know the topic only through the presentation, and some who may be more focused on writing, design, or general public affairs rather than technical grid issues. That mix would help me understand if a policy official, who knows little about the subject would undetstand the presentation, and be able to implement its recommendations. 

The story is meant to reach policymkers who may not know much about interconnection queues before seeing it, but who should leave with a clear sense of why the backlog matters, why PJM is an important case study, and why the issue is bigger than one region. In the interviews, I would hope to learn whether the story feels engaging, understandable, and persuasive enough for this audience, and whether the call to action feels relevant even to people who are encountering the issue for the first time.
